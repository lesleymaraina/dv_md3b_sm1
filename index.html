Encoding Numeric Data for Visualization

  • Encoding : the translation step between numbers and perception

  • Encode numeric data: map quantitative values — measurements, rates, concentrations, durations — onto visual properties that the human eye can perceive and compare

  • For example:

    • Consider a bar chart of mean tumor volume or a scatterplot of gene expression versus dosage, the numbers themselves aren’t visible — what you see are encoded representations

Encoding Numeric Data for Visualization

Encoding properties within the context of plots:

  • Position — where a point or line falls on an axis

  • Length — how tall or long a bar or segment is

  • Area — the size of a shape or bubble

  • Color — hue or intensity representing a scale of values

  • Angle or slope — degree of change or proportion

Each of these properties is a channel through which a numerical difference becomes visually perceptible

Encoding transforms abstract quantities into spatial evidence

Encoding converts quantitative differences into visual differences that the viewer can process quickly and intuitively

Numeric Concept Visual Encoding Perceptual Meaning
Tumor volume (cm^3) Bar height More = larger volume
Time to progression (months) Point position on x-axis Farther right = longer survival
Fold-change in expression Color intensity Darker = higher expression
Hospital cost Circle area Bigger = higher cost

Encoding choices determine accuracy and interpretability

Position and length - key for accurate visual encodings

Area and color - possibly perceptually weak

  • Color intensity depends on context and contrast, not true magnitude

Encoding choices determine accuracy and interpretability

Encoding errors distort perception even when numbers are correct

  • Truncated bars exaggerate small differences

  • Bubble plots overstate change because area grows faster than value

Encoding is a scientific decision

  • The choice of visual form determines whether viewers perceive quantitative truth or visual illusion

Task: Compare blood glucose (mean HbA1c) reduction across diabetes therapies

Plotting Concepts

  • Plot to visualize mean HbA1c reduction across diabetes therapies
  • Each bar represents a treatment arm in a clinical trial
  • Bars encode quantitative values as length from a baseline — ideal for comparing independent groups
  • Axes must start at zero and display negative values

Scatter Plots: Showing Paired or Temporal Magnitudes

Scatter plots are foundational for understanding relationships between two quantitative variables

Each point represents one observation, positioned by its values on two continuous scales

An independent (predictor) variable on the x-axis and a dependent (response) variable on the y-axis

Common Uses in Biomedical Research

  • Pharmacodynamics – Tumor volume vs. drug dosage
  • Epidemiology – BMI vs. systolic blood pressure
  • Genomics – Expression level vs. mutation load

Scatter Plots: Showing Paired or Temporal Magnitudes

Example:

Dose-response relationship: Does increasing drug dose reduce tumor volume in treated subjects?

  • plot shows each patient’s tumor volume (y-axis) against administered dose (x-axis)

  • negative correlation between dose and tumor volume: as dose increases, average tumor volume decreases

  • pattern supports a therapeutic effect of the drug within the tested range — higher doses appear to inhibit tumor growth

Distributions: Visualization Variation in Data

Plots showing distribution gives the reader information beyond a single summary numbers and represents a full range of observed values for a variable

Distribution plots allow the reader to ask questions beyond: What is the average?

Instead:

  • How are values spread out?
  • Are they symmetric or skewed?
  • Are there clusters, gaps, or outliers?

Distributions: Visualization Variation in Data

Plot choices for displaying distributions:

  • Boxplots summarize key percentiles — median, IQR, whiskers, and outliers — giving a compact view of variability

  • Violin plots add the shape of the distribution, showing skewness and bimodality that boxplots alone can miss

  • Ridgelines stack multiple distributions, allowing quick comparison of trends across treatments, timepoints, or biomarkers

In patients with an inflammatory condition such as rheumatoid arthritis, does escalating therapy (Placebo \(\rightarrow\) Combination) lead to lower and more consistent serum cytokine levels?

Plot choice and interpretation:

  • Violin plot displays the full distribution of cytokine levels for each treatment arm
  • Width of each violin: shows where values are most concentrated
  • Box inside violin: shows the median and interquartile range (IQR)

Overall Interpretation: Evidence of potential therapeutic efficacy - as treatment intensity increases, the distribution shifts downward and narrows, indicating both lower median levels and less variability among patients

Summary

  • Turning numbers into perceptible scientific evidence:

    • Encoding allows the reader to easily visualize magnitude, change, and relationship

  • Bars and scatter plots reveal structure and association

    • Plots that help clarify between-group differences and variable relationships

  • Distributions expose the biology of variability

    • Plots like violins and boxplots reveal skew, spread, and outliers

  • Interpreting visualized data is scientific reasoning

    • Visualizing magnitude, correlation, and spread enables inference about mechanisms and treatment effects
